What is being simulated

This tool implements the watercolor model from [1] at reduced resolution for real‑time feedback. For each cell of a 240 × 160 grid we track water saturation, fluid velocity, suspended pigment gk, and deposited pigment dk for three pigments whose K and S coefficients are taken verbatim from Figure 5 of the paper: Quinacridone Rose, Hansa Yellow, and Cerulean Blue.

Each frame runs the paper's main loop — move water, move pigment, transfer pigment, simulate edge flow — using a simplified shallow‑water update inspired by the staggered‑grid scheme of Foster & Metaxas [2] and the divergence‑relaxation approach popularized by Stam [3]. Pigment adsorption/desorption follows §4.5 of [1] directly, with granulation γ modulated by a procedurally generated paper height field. Rendering uses the Kubelka–Munk equations [4] — for every cell, the weighted K and S of the deposited and suspended pigments are composited over white paper to produce the final RGB color, which is what gives the overlapping washes their characteristic luminosity.

The three swatches above are not drawn from a fixed RGB palette — each is rendered with the same KM compositor used on the canvas, so they show the pigments' actual appearance under the simulation, including their differing transparency and undertones.

References

  1. Curtis, C. J., Anderson, S. E., Seims, J. E., Fleischer, K. W., & Salesin, D. H. (1997). Computer-Generated Watercolor. Proceedings of SIGGRAPH '97, pp. 421–430.
  2. Foster, N., & Metaxas, D. (1996). Realistic Animation of Liquids. Graphics Interface '96, pp. 204–212. — Source of the staggered‑grid discretization that Curtis et al. adopt for their shallow‑water solver (§4.3.1).
  3. Stam, J. (1999). Stable Fluids. Proceedings of SIGGRAPH '99, pp. 121–128. — Standard reference for real‑time fluid simulation; we borrow the implicit diffusion and divergence‑projection ideas to keep the sim stable at interactive frame rates.
  4. Kubelka, P. (1954). New Contributions to the Optics of Intensely Light‑Scattering Materials, Part II: Nonhomogeneous Layers. Journal of the Optical Society of America, 44(4), 330–335. — The compositing model used for §5 of [1] and here for the per‑pixel color computation.
  5. Small, D. (1991). Simulating Watercolor by Modeling Diffusion, Pigment, and Paper Fibers. Proceedings of SPIE '91. — The earlier cellular‑automaton predecessor discussed in §1.1 of [1].
